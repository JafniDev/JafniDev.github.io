The Evolution of Pizza Through History

Introduction

Pizza is one of the world's most beloved foods, but its story goes far beyond mozzarella and tomato sauce. From humble origins to international fame, pizza’s history is a fascinating tale of cultural exchange, innovation, and globalization.

01

Ancient Roots: The Origins of Flatbread Dishes

Long before the word “pizza” existed, ancient Egyptians, Greeks, and Romans were baking flatbreads topped with oil, herbs, and cheese. The Greek plakous and Roman picea may be early ancestors of what we now call pizza.

02

Pizza’s Birthplace: Naples, Italy

Modern pizza was born in 18th-century Naples as affordable street food for the working class. These early pizzas were topped with simple ingredients like garlic, salt, lard, and anchovies.

03

The Margherita Pizza and Royal Recognition

In 1889, Queen Margherita of Savoy visited Naples. Chef Raffaele Esposito created a pizza with tomato, mozzarella, and basil—representing the Italian flag’s colors—and named it in her honor: Pizza Margherita.

04

Pizza Crosses the Atlantic

Italian immigrants introduced pizza to the United States in the early 20th century. The first U.S. pizzeria, Lombardi’s, opened in New York City in 1905, and pizza quickly gained popularity in urban immigrant communities.

05

Post-War Popularity Explosion

After World War II, returning American soldiers who had tasted pizza in Italy helped spark a nationwide pizza boom. This led to the rise of major chains like Pizza Hut (1958) and Domino’s (1960), transforming pizza into a mainstream staple.

06

Regional Styles and Global Variations

Neapolitan: Thin, simple, with a charred crust and fresh ingredients

New York-style: Thin, foldable slices with wide appeal

Chicago deep-dish: Thick and hearty, baked in a pan

California: Gourmet and experimental with fresh toppings

Global takes: Sushi pizza (Japan), paneer tikka pizza (India), and more

Pizza has evolved into a global food, adapting to local tastes while maintaining its essential charm. ------------------------------------

07

Pizza in Pop Culture and Economics

Today, pizza is not only food—it’s a cultural icon. It appears in movies, memes, political discussions, and economic reports. Pizza sales generate billions globally, making it a central part of the fast-food economy.

Conclusion

From ancient flatbreads to gourmet pies, pizza’s journey mirrors human history—migration, creativity, and adaptation. No matter where you are in the world, there’s a version of pizza to enjoy—each slice with its own story.

Really Dough?

Really Dough? is a web series from Thrillist where Mark Iacono, a pizza traditionalist, and Scott Wiener, a pizza enthusiast, explore the definition of pizza by trying unconventional toppings and preparations. The show highlights their contrasting views, with Mark adhering to strict rules (sauce, cheese, dough) and Scott embracing creative interpretations. The series delves into whether these experimental pizzas, like kimchi pizza or deep-dish, truly qualify as pizza
